Finding Counties Missed by Infrastructure-Only Medicaid Access Screening

Postpartum Medicaid access is often discussed through coverage policy: eligibility, renewal, and postpartum coverage duration. But coverage continuity also depends on whether people can navigate administrative systems, reach in-person support, access digital tools, travel to care, and find local maternity care infrastructure.

This project develops a county-level screening index to identify where those barriers may overlap.

Research question: Where may postpartum Medicaid populations face greater administrative access barriers to maintaining coverage and navigating support after delivery?

Main Finding

An infrastructure-only screen looks for counties with both major local infrastructure gaps: no Medicaid office and no hospital-based obstetric care. This screen identifies many high-concern counties, but it is incomplete.

665 counties classified as high or highest concern
386 high/highest counties captured by the infrastructure-only screen
279 high/highest counties missed by the infrastructure-only screen

The missed counties were not low-burden outliers. They had an average score of 6.46 and an average contextual support-burden score of 4.56. Most were nonmetro, and large shares had high poverty, high no-internet rates, high disability rates, or high no-vehicle household rates.

The index's main contribution is identifying these otherwise-missed counties and showing why they may still warrant review.

What the Index Adds

The index does not only ask whether a county has both major infrastructure gaps. It asks whether a county has overlapping barriers across three domains:

  1. Administrative access: whether the county has a Medicaid office.
  2. Clinical maternity infrastructure: whether the county has hospital-based obstetric care.
  3. Access-support burden: whether contextual barriers may make administrative or clinical gaps harder to navigate.

This matters because a county with only one infrastructure gap may still face substantial access burden if that gap overlaps with poverty, transportation barriers, digital access barriers, limited English access needs, disability-related support context, reproductive-age population context, or rurality.

How the Score Works

The Postpartum Medicaid Access Barrier Index is a transparent county-level screening score. The theoretical score range is 0-11, and observed county scores ranged from 0 to 10.

  • No Medicaid office in county = 2 points.
  • No hospital-based obstetric care = 2 points.
  • Top quartile poverty rate = 1 point.
  • Top quartile no-vehicle household rate = 1 point.
  • Top quartile no-internet subscription rate = 1 point.
  • Top quartile limited English rate = 1 point.
  • Top quartile disability rate = 1 point.
  • Top quartile female population ages 15-44 rate = 1 point.
  • Nonmetro county status = 1 point.

A Practical Review Sequence

1. Start with highest-concern counties

The first review group is the 56 highest-concern counties. Within that group, 11 counties reached the maximum observed score of 10.

2. Review counties with both infrastructure gaps

Counties with both no Medicaid office and no hospital-based obstetric care are the clearest infrastructure-gap counties.

3. Review one-gap plus high-context counties

The most important added-value group is counties with one direct infrastructure gap plus multiple contextual barriers. These counties may not appear in an infrastructure-only screen, but they may still warrant review.

4. Review contextual-burden watchlist counties

A smaller group of counties reached high concern through contextual burden without either direct infrastructure gap. These counties may warrant review when the goal is to understand broader access-support burden.

Applied Screening Rule

Among the 279 high/highest concern counties missed by the infrastructure-only screen, a rule requiring one infrastructure gap plus at least four contextual flags recovered 265 counties, or 95.0%, with 100.0% precision.

This finding supports the two-layer structure of the index. Direct infrastructure gaps matter, but contextual burden helps identify which one-gap counties may deserve closer review.

Geographic Patterns

High scores were concentrated in the South, parts of the Southwest, and Alaska. Mississippi had the highest average county score and the highest share of high/highest concern counties. Texas had the largest number of high/highest concern counties.

These patterns identify a geography for follow-up analysis using Medicaid enrollment, renewal, churn, coverage continuity, and postpartum care data.

Dashboard Translation

The Power BI dashboard translates the index into a public review tool. Users can examine county-level scores, concern categories, ranked county lists, state comparisons, administrative versus clinical infrastructure gaps, and component-level score drivers.

Bottom Line

The Postpartum Medicaid Access Barrier Index shows that the most obvious infrastructure gaps are only part of the access-burden picture. An infrastructure-only screen captured many high-concern counties, but it missed 279 high/highest concern counties. Most of those missed counties had one infrastructure gap plus multiple contextual barriers.

The central contribution of the index is that it provides a transparent way to identify counties that may be overlooked by simpler Medicaid access screens and to show where additional barriers make those counties worth closer policy and program review.
